# forward

There have been a few times throughout human history that people have come together with the brilliant idea of educating themselves, and their children universally. 
In general, this is met with quite a lot of opposition from men in power. 
And that's pretty much the litmus test for what must be a good idea.

In the late 20th century, it was taken for granted that an American youth would be in school, and so I took for granted that I had kind and well-meaning adults around me, helping to understand a large and foreboding world. 

In fifth grade, my family moved to Japan where I ended up in a small international school called Nishi-Machi.
It had a single computer lab full of Apple IIe's, the computer that was the hallmark of computing education for the few years before the worldwide web changed computing forever.

My teacher, an affable brit, sat us in front of those curved black glass screens, and instructed us to start a program.
In front of me a little 1-bit square with a head, maybe imagined, maybe there, appeared.
A turtle, we were told, a turtle with a pen.

He instructed us to type "forward 20."
I did.
The turtle moved.

My life was changed.

## The youts

I've read a few things about how the youts are going to know less about computers than us millenials. 
This seems fine to me, after all how much do we know about carbourators and dual-brush motors?
But then it's also a bit like saying, the youts are going to know less about guitars.
That sounds much more tragic.

My parents did many things, but one of the things they got right was making sure that us kids were exposed to the whole gambit of human things: sports, art, music, and yes even machines (my mom gets the credit for this one, my dad mostly just cared about sports).
I found interests in a lot of these things, but the one that really stuck was computers. 
And so, since one good turn deserves another, once it was discovered I was headed for nerdom, they took my computer away from me.

It took me close to a decade to get one back.
That's how you stop the youts from learning.

About a decade ago I ran for school board.
This seemed quite awkward to a lot of people since I didn't have any kids.
I guess my liberal Portland compatriots had forgotten that maybe an educated populace was of broader interest than ourselves.

As part of that I met with my state representative who sent me the state budget, and asked me to propose how to reallocate things to better fund the school system.
I'm not sure this challenge was wholly in good faith, but he didn't know he was challenging ol' planetnineisaspaceship. 
If there was money to be found, I was gonna find it.

States spend money on plenty of things, but there are two buckets that dwarf everything else: education and healthcare.
When Medicaid and Medicare started in the 60s, states were on the hook for Medicaid with matching funds from the federal government.
For a plethora of reasons, this Medicaid spending grew year over year. 

The extra money on healthcare had to come from somewhere, and that somewhere was the only bucket big enough to let some of its water go.

I looked at maybe ten states' budgets from around the country, and they all told the same story. 
Education budgets had been eroded year over year, just half a percent or so, to where thirty years later kids were making due with 20% less money, and 20% more kids. 

This blog post is not for going into all of the problems that this is causing, but I will say one of the first things to go were those computer labs with nice shiny macs.
Filling the void are cheap always-online netbooks from the largest spyware company on Earth.
Giving new meaning to tracking students.

Now that every kid was online, every school was poised to have all of their lessons paid into subscription services. 
Just the thing we need as budgets shrink all over the land.
But the real tragedy for our story?

You can't open up a terminal on a chromebook and just write some code.

## Let's get started

I've asked an LLM the following, "In your opinion, what's the easiest way to get a medium sized website online?"
Its response contains the following technologies:

* HTML/CSS/JS, React, Vue, etc.
* Vercel or Netlify
* Git repository
* Node.js, Python, etc.
* Railway or Render 
* Wordpress
* Cloudways or Kinsta
* SSL certificates
* Bluehost with cPanel
* FTP headaches

I've been doing this for three decades, and I don't even know what all these are. 

There are only two professions I know of whose jobs get harder the longer they exist: lawyers and programmers. 
Both of these are largely a result of the combinatorial nature of introduced complexity into the systems they negotiate. 
For the Law this is annoying, but largely necessary since humans are complex, and their cultural systems map to that complexity.
But does this need to be the case for the machines?

You can't even really study law to be a lawyer until graduate school so it makes sense that it starts complicated. 
But you can pop open a computer and start coding stuff long before you have to build something that scales to a million users on the internet. 
Anyone know how to do that?
Anyone remember doing that in school?

First you need a computer.
Not a netbook, but a proper machine, which nowadays can be a raspberry pi that is a quarter of the price as those chromebooks, and doesn't spy on you!

Then you need someone who knows how to program the computer, and is willing to teach the youts. 
If you have all of that bs above, the list of people who are going to meet both of these criteria is small. 

But moving a little turtle with a pen forward?

Well that's just fun.

## Have you ever not followed a recipe?

Is there anything better than not following the rules, and being rewarded with something better for your troubles?
Sure the lego castle set is good as a castle, but its great as the majestic reimagining of a space castle beachfront!
Sometime's tea's better off in a harbor y'know?

Many people learn how to cook by tweaking recipes.
Discovery happens in all fashions, whether dedicated experimentation, or necessary substitution, or serendipitous entropy. 
Sometimes the result is a flop, inedible and literal garbage, and sometimes a triumph.
But the key, a side effect of the ubiquity of food, is the accessibility of these recipes. 

I don't mean being able to find them. 
I mean the fact that most people know what flour is, and if they don't it's easy enough to find since we have food stores all over the place.[^1]

What's the flour for code?
And what's the code store where I can find it?
